All about corals

 

Where are coral reefs found

 
Coral reefs are all over the globe, mostly in warm shallow waters near the equator with the average temperature around 23°c  – 29°c, although the minimum that they can survive at is 18°c.
 

Percipitation

The coral reef biome experiences an average yearly precipitation of 78.75 inches. The yearly average rainfall varies depending upon the location of the coral reef.

For example, the most rainfall on the Great Barrier Reef occurs in the rainy season, which starts in September and ends in March.

How do coral reefs form?

 
Coral polyps secrete skeletons of calcium carbonate (CaCO3) which slowly build structures over time. The way a coral looks depends on the water currents flowing at the time, shaping corals into magnificent shapes and forms.
 

What animals rely on coral reefs?

 
Fish, lobsters, clams, seahorses, sponges, and sea turtles are only some of the thousands of creatures that rely on coral reefs for their survival.

Threats to coral reefs because of climate change

 
A warming ocean causes thermal stress which can cause coral bleaching.
 
When the pH levels go down, the ocean gets more acidic which causes coral bleaching.
 
Changes in precipitation can cause more runoff water, which can wash pollutants into the water and cause coral
bleaching.
 
Changes in storm patterns can cause storm surges, which dramatically wipe out corals
 
Sea level rising can cause sedimentation, which smothers corals.
 
When corals die, fish and other animals that live in and around corals will be left unprotected from predators and leave the coral reef, thus turning it into a ghost town.

How do corals get their algae? (Zooxanthellae)

In asexual reproduction, a part of the coral snaps off, floating to somewhere else in the ocean to land. Usually this is not timed.

 Additionally, corals can obtain zooxanthellae indirectly through the ingestion of fecal matter excreted by corallivores (animals that eat coral) and of animals who have eaten prey with zooxanthellae in their cells (prey such as jellyfish and sea anemones).

In sexual reproduction, the zooxanthellae comes with the egg and sperm in reproduction, and the reproduction is triggered by environmental causes, such as full moon, temperature of the water and several other environmental cues.

Coral Polyps

 
Coral polyps are organisms that live inside and build coral reefs. They have an alage inside them which gives them nutrients and keeps them healthy, fighting viruses and other intruding organisams.
 
Corals feed by extending thier calyx and grabbing floating things like Zooplankton and other microsopic organisams that pass by.
 
When polyps are physically stressed, they contract into their calyx so that virtually no part is exposed above their skeleton. This protects the polyp from predators and the elements. At other times, polyps extend out of the calyx. Most polyps extend the farthest when they feed.
 

Zooxanthellae

 
Zooxanthellae give corals their main source of energy and nutrients using photosynthesis, thus giving corals their distinct colour.
 
In return, coral polyps give zooxanthellae CO2

Coral Bleaching

Coral bleaching happens when coral polyps get stressed by environmental causes (Listed above) they expel their zooxanthellae algae, which is there main source of defence and nutrients, so corals usually die of starvation or disease.
 
Coral polyps may not adapt to the new water temperatures or acidity in time to regain thier alage.

Into the future

  • At present, local human activities, coupled with past thermal stress, threaten an estimated 75 percent of the world’s reefs.
  • By 2030, more than 90% of the world’s reefs will be threatened by local human activities, warming, and acidification, with nearly 60% facing high, very high, or critical threat levels.
  • By 2050, nearly all of the reefs will be threatened, with 75% facing high, very high, or critical threat levels.
  • By 2050 onward, all reefs will be facing the threat of extinction.
